### The "Quadrifold Gates" Strategy
#### Demo
***
- Whenever it's finished and reasonably polished, release a demo of Lumina for free on Steam.
- Rely solely on word of mouth for the demo. This is the first step of the plan, and taking things slow here is wise.
	- If we have any CC channels (Twitch, YT, etc.), bring up the demo's release there.
- Gather feedback from reviews & playtesters, log feedback, adjust accordingly (**with wisdom**).
- Continuously ask trusted people from various backgrounds to testplay once it's testplay-ready.
	- Especially important to do whenever we finish another chapter of the game
- Consider releasing "closed-beta" versions of the different chapters on Patreon
	- Gather feedback from beta testers trying out the different chapters

#### Early Access
***
- Determine final release price
	- Price should be somewhere around **half** of what is perceived to be "full price" at the time of release.
- Release the game in early access, for 50% of projected final price.
	- "Early Access" will be the stage we're at once every major bossfight has been completed and polished, leaving only the final boss and post-game.
- This allows us to gather feedback on all the main parts of the game, garner some initial attention, and polish things up before our major release.
- This also comes with the opportunity to give us a reasonable amount of money to work with up until the final release, which helps in the subsequent steps

#### Pre-release
***
- If we can afford to; create a unique, memorable trailer - on the order of Mercedes-Benz' chicken commercial. 
	- **DO NOT** PUBLISH THIS TRAILER AS AN AD!! 
	- Upload it to Youtube under Esoterica channel instead. Indie video game ads will **not** have the desired effect (do keep an eye on the overall gaming sphere to ensure this remains true).
- Give free key for the **full game** to mid-size streamers (50~800 viewers), ask them for honest opinions. 
	- Mid-size streamers are less likely to be inundated with offers from big names that we can't compete with, and would be more likely to give the game a chance. They still have enough of an audience that it can fuel word of mouth. There's a larger quantity of them, so more opportunities to ask and be accepted.
	- If they like the game, offer them free Steam keys to give away to their viewers. 
		- 5% of their avg viewcount in keys (estimate)
	- Small streamers that seem like cool people are also a perfectly valid option.
	- **Make sure these streamers match our target audience!**
	- Take feedback graciously! **Don't argue** with them, **just listen and validate**, and apply the feedback wisely.
- Give free key for the *ull game** to mid-to-large gaming video essayists, and ask them for feedback.*f
	- Explicitly state that we aren't asking them to make a video about the game, just that we value their criticism and would like their feedback so we can improve the game.
	- Ask them to be **brutally honest** with their feedback.
		- This (hopefully) signals to them that we're acting in good faith.
	- If we have the money to afford it, we can also frame it as a sponsorship deal.
		- In this scenario, we would require a video. However, we would still ask them to not pull any punches. We could also ask that, if their review/opinion is very negative, they contact us first before releasing a video about it.
		- *Let them know we're open for feedback!!* We don't just want the publicity! These guys have good takes and are passionate about the medium! They could seriously help us improve the game before release.
	- Joseph Anderson, Jacob Geller, Daryl Talks Games, NeverKnowsBest, etc.

#### Release
***
- BOGO deal for all purchases made through Steam. 
	- Duration of BOGO is up for discussion; could be a pre-order bonus, pre-order + release day, or some other timeframe. 
	- Price should stay full, but the free copies of the game allow for the game to spread.
	- *I know this seems counter-intuitive!* 
		- For every sale we make, we're essentially depriving ourselves of an additional sale, while also enabling resellers to sell our game on other platforms for less money than we are.
		- *This is the point!* We don't want to maximize sales! We want to maximize our fanbase! We're starting out as a completely unknown company. Even if we were motivated by pure greed, earning the goodwill of our customers is paramount. However, Esoterica stands for quality and high art above all else. There is no better way to communicate this to our target audience than by having such an approach.
		- If we don't do things this way, we'll just be yet another indie company in a sea of others. To distinguish ourselves, we must take risky, but calculated steps.
- Monitor resellers of free keys, but pretend we don't notice them
	- C&D their platform if they get out of hand, and revoke their keys if it gets to this point.


### Additional Considerations & Thoughts
- Keep tabs on piracy. If a pirated copy of the game emerges, upload a legitimate copy ANONYMOUSLY. If someone is going to pirate the game, we want them to be able to play it without hassle or viruses. Players without money are valid too!
- This plan can ONLY work if the game looks good (grabs attention) and is good (retains attention). We're not going to use any cheap tactics to achieve these goals. We're here to make games; making money is *extremely* important, but still secondary. We can't ever get our priorities mixed up.
- Any amount of social media attention would be good, as long as it's not overly negative. Pissing off unreasonable people with large audiences is particularly effective, but we're not interested in explicitly trying to do something like that. Any amount of discussion (preferably positive/constructive) would help.
- DON'T BE OVER-EAGER. Asking too many CC's at once will just lead to them bringing you up negatively with each other. Optics are everything in marketing, and the last thing we want is to look desperate; but we also want to have as many eyes on the game as possible, to increase the chance that the target audience actually learns it exists.
- This approach effectively trades sales for marketing costs. By giving away more free copies, we don't have the upfront cost of marketing, but we still get eyes on the game; meaning we need less of a budget to get off the ground.
- ALL OF THIS IS SO RISKY!!!!! (then again, we're making games, so, lol.)
